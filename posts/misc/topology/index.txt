13:Tcb3,<p>I hadn’t known Topology until a friend of mine gave me a quiz related to it. Though I still have no idea about the mathematical stuff, it has been drawing my attention due to some of its interesting facts.</p>
<h3 id="what-is-topology">What is Topology?</h3>
<p>I found a definition on <a href="https://www.livescience.com">livescience.com</a> (more understandable than on Wiki)</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em><strong>Topology</strong></em> is a branch of mathematics that describes mathematical <em><strong>spaces</strong></em>, in particular the properties that stem from a <em><strong>space’s shape</strong></em>. Many of the shapes topologists deal with are incredibly strange, so much so that practically all everyday objects such as bowls and pets and trees make up a small minority. The word “topology” derives from the Greek words for place (topos) and study (-logy).</p>
</blockquote>
<p>For instance, under Topology’s perspective, a <em>donut</em> and a <em>coffee cup</em> are the same since they have a similar shape (a solid object with a hole). Similarly, a rubik’s cube is just a different version of a ball (by somehow, we can mould a cubic object into a sphere).</p>
<iframe width="700" height="350" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/4iHjt2Ovqag" frameborder="0" gesture="media" allow="encrypted-media" allowfullscreen></iframe>
<p>Another example is the DNA. After a DNA molecule unwinds, <em>it resembles a ladder</em>. So what are the implications here? Well, basically it means: <em><strong>“with the same transformation to a DNA, we may get the same result to a ladder and vice versa”</strong></em>. To be specific, we can shrink a ladder with the same principle a huge DNA molecule winds itself into a smaller one.</p>
<p>For more typical examples, just have a look at the following videos:</p>
<iframe width="700" height="350" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/BVsIAa2XNKc" frameborder="0" gesture="media" allow="encrypted-media" allowfullscreen></iframe>
<iframe width="700" height="350" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/oHGlDCo3eMk" frameborder="0" gesture="media" allow="encrypted-media" allowfullscreen></iframe>
<p><span class="figcaption">We could transform a rubber band to a variety of shapes without cutting or glueing it. There is a sub-branch of Topology studying this kind of homomorphism: “Knot Theory”</span></p>
<h3 id="topology---philosophy">Topology - Philosophy</h3>
<p>From my point of view, there are 2 pieces of philosophy apparently exposed:</p>
<p><strong>[1] There must be a simple instance of a complex object.</strong> Therefore, a complicated problem could be reduced to a simpler one.</p>
<p><strong>[2] Since objects are in analogy under some aspects of consideration, the similar solutions may lead to the similar results.</strong></p>
<h3 id="why-does-it-draw-my-attention">Why does it draw my attention?</h3>
<ul>
<li>First of all, I love visual objects.</li>
<li>Secondly, I love analogy.</li>
<li>And finally, I love simplicity.</li>
</ul>
